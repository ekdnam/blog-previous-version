I have been thinking a lot about building products recently and also spoke with some industry veterans. Jotting down my thoughts.

The current jump in AI capabilities has been nothing short of groundbreaking. We see new jumps in capabilities everyday. 

A good way to see where the general public is leaning is to look at Polymarket.

As we see, X.ai had a good lead up until yesterday, when Deepmind released their new model yesterday which led to tectonic shift in public perception.

As we see, public perception is pretty flaky, the consumer will automatically shift to the best alternative once it becomes available. This not only applies to the best models out there, but also applications built on those models. For example: I have been a big cursor fan (accidentally met one of the cofounders in SF at an Italian restaurant) and I still use it everyday. However, there was a massive vibe shift on twitter/X and windsurf started gaining traction. Now there are a lot of people who have "shifted" their sides from Cursor to Windsurf. This again shows how a lot of people are flaky. They just want what's best and are not tied to a product.

Since this is the case, I think startups that have some success face a few dilemmas. They have to keep the cost to switch low, since anyone might want to use their product. However, this will also mean a few people might also switch to their competitors at a moment's notice. 

Thus I think customer differentiation will be primary. You should find your own niche and try to solve in that niche specifically. I think this is typical product management, however, a lot of people might be forgetting that these days, especially since AI models can basically do anything.

Additionally I feel the more unique and niche problem you will solve, the less competition you will have.

I was speaking with the CTO of a startup recently and he brought about a good point. People are trying to pigeonhole AI into their product, leading to possible gain in valuation and marketing, however leading to loss in customer experience because the product is very generic and rather does not fit well into solving the actual problem a customer might have. This might also be the reason why at large AI adoption into actual products is very less, because most don't try to actively enhance user experience and actually solve problems. Because at the end, the customer pays with their wallet. Even though some one might say oh this is nice product! unless they actually pay for it it has no use.

We can also talk about CAC and distribution over here. Many AI companies lose money while running their models basically subsidizing them using VC money so as to gain customer traction. I believe this leads to high CAC.

These AI models are like supernovaes. They have absolutely wonderful and have tremendous energy and capabilities. But unless we know how to use them and bring value to customers, its practically useless.

Even though ChatGPT can do so many things, from my anecdata people mostly use it for coding. / message rewriting / asking general knowledge questions. I am not talking about the people who are actively following AI,but mostly about the average person off the street.

This shows that the industry as a whole has a lot of potential to work on customer problems using AI, because a large part of the world  does not know much about it yet.

I believe in this AI revolution we should focus on solving specific customer problems rather than generic pigeonholing.
